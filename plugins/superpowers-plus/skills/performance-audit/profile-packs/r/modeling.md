## Statistical modeling & forecasting (lm/glm / survival / forecast·fable / tidymodels)

> Scope: base `stats` regression (`lm`/`glm` and their `model.matrix`/IRLS machinery), large-data alternatives (`biglm`/`speedglm`/`mgcv::bam`), survival/reliability (`survival::coxph`, `survreg`, `fitdistrplus`), classical and many-series forecasting (`forecast`, `fable`/`fabletools`), and the resampling frameworks (`caret`, `tidymodels`/`tune`, `glmnet`, `ranger`, R-`xgboost`/`lightgbm`) — the stack behind a utility's load forecasting, equipment-failure modeling, and econometric regression on large meter/SCADA datasets. This is a **performance** lens (fit time, predict latency, memory, resampling throughput), not a model-accuracy lens. Recurring themes: **don't materialize the full dense design matrix when you can avoid it**, **fit the structure once and reuse it** rather than refitting per backtest origin / per fold, **count your threads** (engine threads × BLAS × parallel workers is the R oversubscription trap), and **cross the R↔C boundary in one bulk call** instead of per-row. The model fit itself is BLAS-bound and resampling is the parallelism surface — both live in the **Runtime & copy-on-modify notes** in `../r.md`, referenced throughout.

- **`lm`/`glm` materialize the full dense `model.matrix` and factor/interaction expansion blows it up.** Both build a complete `n × p` design matrix from the formula before fitting (QR for `lm`, IRLS over that matrix for `glm`). A factor with k levels expands to k−1 dummy columns, and a `*`/`:` interaction multiplies level counts — `feeder * hour_of_day * season` over thousands of feeders is a combinatorial column explosion that dominates both memory and the QR/IRLS cost long before N matters. Before assuming the bottleneck is N, count the columns the formula actually generates (`ncol(model.matrix(formula, data))`). Cross-reference the **`memory`** lane in `../r.md` (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Past large N, base `lm`/`glm` hold the whole matrix when `biglm`/`speedglm`/`bam` need not.** `biglm`/`bigglm` accumulate only the sufficient statistics (cross-product matrices) over data fed in chunks via a `data` function, so memory stays roughly constant regardless of N and the data never has to fit in RAM at once; `speedglm`/`speedlm` similarly fit via updating cross-products (with `shglm` for chunked GLM) and lean on the optimized BLAS. `mgcv::bam` is the GAM analog for big-N additive load models. These are architectural alternatives — flag them when the profiled cost is base `glm` on a many-row meter table, not when N is modest (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **A sparse design fed through the dense `model.matrix` path wastes memory it never needed.** When the design is mostly indicator columns (many dummies — one-hot meter/substation IDs, calendar effects), `Matrix::sparse.model.matrix` / `MatrixModels` keep it sparse, which the base man page itself flags as "more efficient in large dimensions." The base formula interface always densifies; reaching `glmnet` (which takes `Matrix` sparse input directly) or a sparse-aware fitter avoids materializing a huge mostly-zero block. Check the zero-fraction of the design before assuming dense is required (cross-reference **`memory`** in `../r.md`) (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Refitting from scratch at every rolling/expanding-window backtest origin.** `forecast::tsCV` re-runs the supplied `forecastfunction` — and thus re-estimates the model (e.g. a fresh `auto.arima`) — at *every* forecast origin; over a long half-hourly load series that is O(n_origins) full MLE fits and is frequently the dominant cost of an evaluation. The cheaper pattern fixes parameters once and re-filters forward: re-estimate periodically rather than per step, reuse a fitted `ets`/`Arima` object via `model=` to skip order selection on each refit, and prefer `window=` (rolling, bounded) only when the model genuinely needs it. This is the R analog of statsmodels `.append`/`.extend` (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Forecasting many independent series in a serial loop instead of batched.** Per-feeder / per-meter forecasts are independent, so a hand-rolled `for`/`lapply` over series leaves cores idle. `fable`/`fabletools` `model()` fits one or more specifications across *all* keyed series of a tsibble in one call (producing a mable), and parallelizes simply by setting a `future::plan()` beforehand. But the speedup collides with the oversubscription trap: each worker's `lm.fit`/`crossprod` spawns its own BLAS thread pool, so `workers × BLAS_threads` can exceed cores and thrash — cap BLAS to 1 in workers or cap workers accordingly. Cross-reference **Runtime & copy-on-modify notes** (BLAS oversubscription) in `../r.md` (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **`coxph(ties="exact")` where Efron suffices — orders-of-magnitude cost.** `coxph` defaults to `ties="efron"` (accurate and efficient); `"breslow"` is cheaper but coarser; `"exact"` enumerates subsets of the risk set and the man page is explicit that with even ~15 events among ~180 at risk the combinations exceed 10^43, making it "insufferably long" — and worse with (start, stop) interval data. On utility failure data where many assets are censored/fail at the same coarse timestamp (daily), the tie count is high and the method choice is the cost lever, not the row count. Note multi-state models default to `breslow`. Reach for `"exact"` only when ties are few and exactness is genuinely required (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Cox post-fit cost: baseline hazard and concordance over a large fleet.** `survfit`/`basehaz` over a held-out asset population and `predict(type="survival")` across many rows are their own cost beyond the fit; concordance over comparable pairs grows steeply with the evaluation-set size, so on a smart-meter / fleet-scale holdout the metric can outweigh the fit. Predict for the whole population in one `newdata` call and subsample (or stratify) the concordance evaluation rather than scoring asset-by-asset. The same "one bulk call" applies to `survreg`/`fitdistrplus` reliability fits — fit per asset-group in a parallelized map, not a serial loop, and pin known parameters to shrink each optimization (cross-reference **`concurrency`** in `../r.md`) (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **`predict()` re-derives the model frame/terms from `newdata` every call — so don't call it per row.** `predict.lm`/`predict.glm` rebuild the design matrix from `newdata` using the stored `terms` object on each invocation; calling it once per meter inside a loop pays that frame construction and R↔C crossing per row, whereas one `predict(fit, newdata = all_rows)` builds the matrix once and runs the vectorized kernel. For utility-scale scoring (every meter, every interval) this is a large latency gap, and it is the model-scoring instance of the R↔C-boundary point in **Runtime & copy-on-modify notes** in `../r.md`. For `glm`, also pass `type="response"`/`"link"` deliberately — the wrong one forces extra transform work (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Resampling cost is CV × grid × per-fit — parallelize the right level and cache the recipe.** `caret::train` and tidymodels `tune_grid` multiply folds by grid points by per-fit cost. Both parallelize across resamples once a backend is registered (caret via `foreach`; tune via a `future::plan()`/`mirai`, foreach now deprecated). The lever is `control_grid(parallel_over=)`: `"resamples"` (default) reuses one preprocessed recipe per resample, while `"everything"` re-runs preprocessing for each model candidate — faster only when preprocessing is cheap. `tune_grid` already nests model params under each unique recipe to avoid recomputing an expensive recipe per grid point; the *submodel trick* (`multi_predict`, e.g. many tree counts or `glmnet` lambdas from one fit) collapses a grid dimension entirely. Reach for these before adding workers (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **PSOCK workers copy the data per worker — watch memory and the worker count.** caret/tidymodels PSOCK clusters send a full copy of the data to each worker (the caret docs note five workers means six copies in RAM), so on a large training matrix parallelism can degrade rather than help, and gains plateau well before "all cores" (caret cites diminishing returns past ~7 workers; nested `train`-inside-`rfe` can spawn P² processes if both parallelize). On Linux a forking plan shares pages copy-on-write and sidesteps the copy; PSOCK also needs `pkg=` in `control_grid` so workers load dependencies. Match worker count to data size and memory, not core count alone (cross-reference **`memory`** and **`concurrency`** in `../r.md`) (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **Engine thread args default to all cores and stack multiplicatively with workers and BLAS.** `ranger(num.threads=)`, R-`xgboost`(`nthread=`), and `lightgbm`(`num_threads=`) each default to *all available cores/threads* (LightGBM `0` = OpenMP default; XGBoost = max if unset). Run one of these inside a parallel `tune_grid`/`caret` resample loop, or alongside a BLAS-heavy fit, and you get `engine_threads × workers × BLAS_threads` — far exceeding cores, thrashing caches and context switches. Set engine threads explicitly (LightGBM docs: use *real* cores not hyperthreads, and don't oversize for small data) and divide the budget across the layers. This is the engine instance of the BLAS-oversubscription point in **Runtime & copy-on-modify notes** in `../r.md` (verify against the currency brief for your version).

- **The fit itself is BLAS-bound, and the reference BLAS is an order of magnitude slow.** `lm.fit`, `crossprod`, and the matrix solves under `glm`/`coxph`/`glmnet` go through the linked BLAS; a stock reference BLAS is dramatically slower than OpenBLAS/MKL/Accelerate for the same call — confirm the linkage before chasing micro-optimizations (`sessionInfo()` / `extSoftVersion()`). Within the fit, prefer `crossprod(X)` over `t(X) %*% X`: the former is a single dedicated symmetric-product BLAS call, the latter materializes the transpose and does a general matmul. The BLAS-linkage and oversubscription realities are the central content of **Runtime & copy-on-modify notes** in `../r.md` — defer there for the engine details (verify against the currency brief for your version).

## Sources

Doc pages fetched and verified for this module:

- R `stats::lm` (stat.ethz.ch/R-manual/R-devel/library/stats/html/lm.html): builds model frame → `model.matrix` → QR via `lm.fit`; `model`/`x`/`y`/`qr` retention flags; points to `biglm` for large data.
- R `stats::glm` (stat.ethz.ch/R-manual/R-devel/library/stats/html/glm.html): `glm.fit` default uses IRLS/IWLS over the full `n × p` design matrix; `x`/`y` retention; recommends `bigglm` for large N.
- R `stats::model.matrix` (.../model.matrix.html): factor → k−1 dummy expansion, interaction expansion, `assign`/`contrasts` attributes; `Matrix::sparse.model.matrix` "more efficient in large dimensions."
- R `stats::predict.lm` (.../predict.lm.html): reconstructs the design matrix from `newdata` via the stored terms each call; rank-deficiency/estimability note.
- `biglm` CRAN reference manual (cran.r-project.org/web/packages/biglm/biglm.pdf): biglm/bigglm accumulate cross-product sufficient statistics over chunks via a `data` function, bounded (≈constant) memory independent of N.
- `speedglm` CRAN page (cran.r-project.org/web/packages/speedglm/index.html): speedlm/speedglm fit large lm/glm via updating algorithms / chunked processing without holding the full matrix; depends on Matrix/biglm.
- `survival::coxph` (stat.ethz.ch/R-manual/R-devel/library/survival/html/coxph.html): `ties` default `efron` vs `breslow` vs `exact`; exact enumerates subsets (>10^43 for ~15/180), "insufferably long," worse with (start,stop); multi-state defaults to breslow.
- `forecast::tsCV` (pkg.robjhyndman.com/forecast/reference/tsCV.html): re-runs `forecastfunction` (re-estimating) at each rolling origin; `window=NULL` expanding vs numeric rolling; explicit computational-cost note.
- `fabletools::model` (fabletools.tidyverts.org/reference/model.html): fits specs across all keyed series → mable; parallelizes by setting `future::plan()`; `progressr` progress; `.safely`.
- tidymodels `tune` optimizations vignette (tune.tidymodels.org/articles/extras/optimizations.html): future/mirai (foreach deprecated), `parallel_over` "resamples" vs "everything", recipe recomputation avoidance, submodel trick (`multi_predict`), PSOCK `pkg=`.
- caret parallel-processing (topepo.github.io/caret/parallel-processing.html): foreach across resamples, `allowParallel`, N workers = N+1 data copies in RAM, diminishing returns past ~7 workers, nested P² warning.
- `glmnet` vignette (glmnet.stanford.edu/articles/glmnet.html): full lambda path in one coordinate-descent call (warm starts/strong rules), `cv.glmnet(parallel=TRUE)` with registered backend, dense/sparse `Matrix` input.
- `ranger` reference (rdocumentation.org/packages/ranger): `num.threads` defaults to all available CPUs; multithreading disabled under regularization.
- XGBoost parameters (xgboost.readthedocs.io/en/stable/parameter.html): `nthread` defaults to max available threads if unset; prefer the Booster/DMatrix `nthread` over the global to avoid library conflicts.
- LightGBM parameters (lightgbm.readthedocs.io/en/latest/Parameters.html): `num_threads=0` → OpenMP default; set to *real* cores not hyperthreads; don't oversize for small data; don't change mid-training when other packages run jobs.

> Not separately verified against a primary page (flagged for the currency check): (1) the precise **scaling order of `coxph` concordance evaluation** with holdout size — the man page documents tie-method cost but not the concordance complexity; the bullet asserts only "grows steeply with evaluation-set size" and prescribes subsampling, which holds regardless of the exact exponent. (2) **fork-vs-PSOCK copy-on-write** behavior for tidymodels/caret workers is a general R-platform fact (forked workers share pages COW on Unix), not stated on the caret/tune pages fetched — phrased as platform guidance, not a framework guarantee.
